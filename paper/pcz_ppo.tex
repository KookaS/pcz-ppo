% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
PPO with GAE is the dominant on-policy algorithm for continuous and discrete control. When environments produce composite rewards --- a weighted sum of heterogeneous signals with different scales and frequencies --- aggregate normalization allows high-magnitude components to dominate the learning signal, destabilising training and causing catastrophic policy failures. We adapt the per-component normalization idea from GDPO~\cite{shao2024gdpo}, originally proposed for critic-free GRPO in language-model alignment, to standard PPO+GAE with a learned value function. Our method, \textbf{PCZ-PPO}, decomposes the reward into $K$ independently normalizable components, z-normalizes each across the rollout buffer, recombines via environment-defined weights, and feeds the result into standard GAE with a single scalar critic; this decouples reward \emph{scale} (equalized by normalization) from \emph{priority} (preserved by weights). \textbf{Primary finding --- deployment reliability:} at plateau horizon (4M steps, $n{=}\cnum{cfail_bw_n}$ seeds each), PCZ-PPO records zero catastrophic failures (eval mean ${<}0$) where PPO incurs them regularly: BipedalWalker $K{=}3$: $\cnum{cfail_bw_pcz_pct}$\% vs $\cnum{cfail_bw_ppo_pct}$\% failure rate; HalfCheetah $K{=}2$: $\cnum{cfail_hc_pcz_pct}$\%$\dagger$ vs $\cnum{cfail_hc_ppo_pct}$\% ($\dagger$one shared-instability seed). \textbf{Operating envelope --- K-scaling:} on the LunarLander wrapper family at 500k, PCZ-PPO's cross-seed mean is approximately invariant across $K{\in}\{4,6,8\}$ (range ${\approx}20$ points) while PPO collapses at $K{=}8$ (range ${>}100$); all three $K$-values survive Holm--Bonferroni at $\alpha{=}0.05$ (Holm $p{=}\cnum{k4_holm_p},\cnum{k6_holm_p},\cnum{k8_holm_p}$; $d{=}\cnum{k4_welch_d},\cnum{k6_welch_d},\cnum{k8_welch_d}$; BCa 95\% CIs exclude zero: $\cnum{k4_welch_ci}$, $\cnum{k6_welch_ci}$, $\cnum{k8_welch_ci}$). \textbf{Where it hurts:} at $K{=}8$/4M, PCZ-PPO is \emph{worse} than PPO under any non-heterogeneous weight schedule (equal: $\Delta{=}\cnum{rr16_equal_k8_4M_delta}$; flat: $\Delta{=}\cnum{rr16_flat_k8_4M_delta}$). We frame the contribution as \textbf{deployment reliability and sample efficiency under heterogeneous strictly-positive weights at modest horizons}, not a higher performance ceiling. Cross-environment evidence: BipedalWalker win; HalfCheetah and Reacher parity; synthetic trading null in both scale variants; LLM single-step preference alignment null. Ablations show per-component decomposition, post-normalization weights, advantage whitening, and a learned critic are all required; PCZ-PPO requires no architectural changes to PPO --- only a preprocessing step on the reward buffer.
\end{abstract}

% 1. INTRODUCTION
% ============================================================
\section{Introduction}

Reinforcement learning agents trained with PPO~\cite{schulman2017ppo} commonly face environments where the reward function is a weighted sum of multiple signals with different scales, frequencies, and semantic meanings. In robotics, a continuous distance-to-goal signal coexists with sparse binary grasp success and constraint-violation penalties. In autonomous driving, dense lane-keeping rewards dwarf rare but safety-critical collision penalties. In game AI, score-based rewards overwhelm auxiliary shaping signals.

When these heterogeneous signals are summed into a single scalar and normalized globally---the standard practice via VecNormalize~\cite{raffin2021sb3} or advantage whitening---the dominant component controls the running statistics, and the agent struggles to learn from subdominant signals. We call this \emph{variance hijacking}: the highest-variance component captures the normalization scale, suppressing all others.

The GDPO paper~\cite{shao2024gdpo} identified this problem in multi-reward RLHF for large language models and proposed per-reward decoupled normalization for GRPO~\cite{shao2024grpo}, a critic-free algorithm. However, GRPO lacks temporal credit assignment---it uses Monte-Carlo returns without a learned value baseline---making it less suited for sequential decision-making tasks with long horizons and dense per-step rewards.

This paper presents \textbf{PCZ-PPO} (Per-Component Z-normalization for PPO), which takes the per-component normalization idea from GDPO and applies it within the standard PPO+GAE framework, retaining the critic for temporal credit assignment. The distinct name from GDPO emphasises the architectural difference: GDPO targets critic-free GRPO, while PCZ-PPO operates with a single scalar critic, GAE advantages, and a clipped surrogate loss. The matched $2{\times}2$ factorial \{critic, no critic\}$\times$\{aggregate, per-component z-norm\} disentangles the two axes (§\ref{sec:variance_hijacking}, §\ref{sec:ablation}): per-component normalization helps PPO at the headline cell but \emph{collapses} when the critic is removed (D1 vs A1, ${\sim}400$-point drop). The LLM $2{\times}2$ factorial (§\ref{sec:llm_alignment}, regime 4) is null in both critic and critic-free single-step preference alignment. Our contributions:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
\item We bridge per-component reward normalization from critic-free settings (GDPO, APEX~\cite{apex2024}) to single-critic PPO+GAE, with post-aggregation advantage whitening as a stabiliser (ablation A6 in §\ref{sec:ablation} shows whitening is required to keep cross-seed variance bounded at $K{=}4$).
\item We provide a systematic K-scaling sweep at $K{\in}\{2,4,6,8\}$ on LunarLander (Table~\ref{tab:kscaling}): at 500k, PCZ-PPO's mean is approximately invariant across $K{\in}\{4,6,8\}$ (range ${\approx}20$ points) while PPO is unstable (range ${>}100$ points). Under Holm--Bonferroni at $n{=}\cnum{k4_pcz_seeds},\cnum{k6_pcz_seeds},\cnum{k8_pcz_seeds}$ all three $K$-values survive at $\alpha{=}0.05$ (Holm $p{=}\cnum{k4_holm_p},\cnum{k6_holm_p},\cnum{k8_holm_p}$ at $K{=}4,6,8$; §\ref{app:stats}). Best-of-best HP search narrows the canonical-cosine gap from $\cnum{k4_delta}$ to $\cnum{w7_delta_best}$ (peak-vs-peak, Table~\ref{tab:bestofbest}, Figure~\ref{fig:hp_fairness}), so part of the canonical gap is a tuning artifact. At 4M, PPO narrows but does not close the $K{=}4$ gap ($\Delta{=}\cnum{w10_k4_4M_delta}$, variance ratio $\cnum{w10_k4_4M_var_ratio}{\times}$); at $K{=}8$/4M PCZ loses under non-heterogeneous weight schedules and only beats PPO with hand-designed heterogeneous strictly-positive weights (§\ref{sec:limitations}, Figure~\ref{fig:learning_curves_4M}).
\item We present a comprehensive ablation suite isolating the contribution of per-component decomposition (A1 vs A7), advantage whitening (A1 vs A6), explicit weights after z-norm (A1 vs equal-weight PCZ), output-side vs input-side normalization (A1 vs PopArt), multi-head value decomposition (A1 vs C5), and the critic-normalization interaction (D1 closes the $2{\times}2$ factorial: removing the critic from PCZ-PPO collapses A1's $\cnum{ablA1_stat}$ to $\cnum{ablD1_stat}$).
\item We evaluate across 6+ environments with varying reward structures, identifying \emph{both} success and failure regimes: PCZ-PPO helps with heterogeneous-scale densely-firing components (LunarLander wrappers, BipedalWalker terminal-crash) and does \emph{not} help with homogeneous-scale dense components (HalfCheetah, Reacher, homogeneous trading) or with sparse-event components even at large scale spread (heterogeneous-scale trading with terminal bankruptcy, §\ref{sec:trading}). Two failure modes --- homogeneous scales and sparse events --- are documented explicitly.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}

\subsection{Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)}

PPO~\cite{schulman2017ppo} optimizes a clipped surrogate objective:
\begin{equation}
L^{\text{PPO}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t \left[ \min \left( r_t(\theta) \hat{A}_t, \; \text{clip}\left(r_t(\theta), 1{-}\epsilon, 1{+}\epsilon\right) \hat{A}_t \right) \right]
\end{equation}
where $r_t(\theta) = \pi_\theta(a_t|s_t) / \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a_t|s_t)$ is the probability ratio and $\hat{A}_t$ is the advantage estimated via GAE~\cite{schulman2015gae}:
\begin{equation}
\hat{A}_t = \sum_{l=0}^{T-t-1} (\gamma \lambda)^l \delta_{t+l}, \quad \delta_t = r_t + \gamma V_\phi(s_{t+1}) - V_\phi(s_t)
\end{equation}
with learned value function $V_\phi$ and bias-variance tradeoff parameter $\lambda \in [0,1]$.

\subsection{GRPO and GDPO}

GRPO~\cite{shao2024grpo} eliminates the critic, computing advantages as z-scores within a sampled group: $\hat{A}_i = (R_i - \mu_G) / \sigma_G$. GDPO~\cite{shao2024gdpo} addresses a failure mode when multiple reward signals are combined: when distinct rewards $R^{(1)}, \ldots, R^{(K)}$ are summed before group normalization, the z-score computation collapses different reward combinations into identical advantage values (\emph{variance hijacking}). GDPO normalizes each reward component independently before combining:
\begin{equation}
\hat{A}_i^{(k)} = \frac{R_i^{(k)} - \mu_j(R_j^{(k)})}{\sigma_j(R_j^{(k)})}, \quad \hat{A}_i = \text{normalize}\!\left(\sum_{k=1}^{K} \hat{A}_i^{(k)}\right)
\end{equation}

Both GRPO and GDPO are critic-free---they lack temporal credit assignment through a learned value function.

\subsection{Variance Hijacking: A Proportionality Argument}
\label{sec:variance_hijacking}

Consider a composite reward $r_t = \sum_{k=1}^{K} w_k r_t^{(k)}$ where component $k^*$ has $\sigma^{(k^*)} \gg \sigma^{(k)}$ for all $k \neq k^*$. Under aggregate normalization (VecNormalize), the running standard deviation is dominated by $k^*$:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{\text{agg}} \approx w_{k^*} \sigma^{(k^*)}, \quad r_{\text{norm}} = \frac{\sum_k w_k r_t^{(k)}}{w_{k^*} \sigma^{(k^*)}}
\end{equation}
All subdominant components contribute $\frac{w_k \sigma^{(k)}}{w_{k^*} \sigma^{(k^*)}} \ll 1$ to the normalized signal. We call this \emph{variance hijacking}: the highest-variance component captures the normalization scale, suppressing all others regardless of their assigned weights. The problem worsens with $K$ because the probability that at least one component dominates the aggregate variance increases. We note this is a proportionality argument under the dominance assumption $\sigma^{(k^*)} \gg \sigma^{(k)}$; formal finite-sample convergence bounds are outside the scope of this paper.

Our working hypothesis: per-component normalization amplifies noise in the reward signal, and a critic baseline is necessary to absorb it. Two pieces of evidence (full discussion in §\ref{sec:ablation}). First, the gym $2{\times}2$ factorial \{critic, no critic\}$\times$\{aggregate, per-component running z-norm\} (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}): A1 (per-component, critic) $\mathbf{\cnum{ablA1_stat}}$ ($n{=}\cnum{ablA1_seeds}$) vs A7 (aggregate, critic) $\cnum{ablA7_stat}$ ($n{=}\cnum{ablA7_seeds}$) shows PCZ helps with critic ($\Delta{=}\cnum{abl_decomp_delta}$); A1 vs D1 (PCZ, no critic) $\cnum{ablD1_stat}$ ($n{=}\cnum{ablD1_seeds}$) shows PCZ collapses without it (${\sim}400$-point drop); C1 (aggregate, no critic) $\cnum{ablC1_stat}$ ($n{=}\cnum{ablC1_seeds}$) closes the factorial. PCZ \emph{attenuates} noise relative to raw (raw/PCZ ${\approx}\cnum{rr4_raw_over_pcz_ratio}{\times}$) but \emph{amplifies} relative to aggregate z-norm (PCZ/znorm $\approx\cnum{rr4_pcz_over_znorm_ratio}{\times}$ overall; $\cnum{rr4_k4_pcz_over_znorm_ratio}{\times}$/$\cnum{rr4_k6_pcz_over_znorm_ratio}{\times}$/$\cnum{rr4_k8_pcz_over_znorm_ratio}{\times}$ at $K{=}4$/$6$/$8$); the per-component pipeline preserves an order of magnitude more variance in the GAE input, which the critic absorbs.

\section{Related Work}

Per-component reward processing in actor-critic settings spans three lineages, each with limitations that PCZ-PPO addresses.

\textbf{Critic-free group normalization.}
GDPO~\cite{shao2024gdpo}, APEX~\cite{apex2024}, and MO-GRPO~\cite{mogrpo2024} implement per-component normalization in GRPO/flow settings without a learned value function. These methods solve variance hijacking but sacrifice temporal credit assignment, limiting applicability to sequential MDPs with dense per-step rewards.

\textbf{Exploration per-stream normalization ($K{=}2$).}
RND~\cite{burda2018rnd} separates rewards into extrinsic and intrinsic streams, normalizing the intrinsic reward with running statistics and using \emph{separate value heads} per stream. This establishes the precedent for per-stream normalization in PPO but is limited to $K{=}2$ with dual critics, motivated by exploration bonus calibration rather than general multi-component variance decoupling.

\textbf{Multi-objective PPO with multi-head critics.}
GCR-PPO~\cite{gcrppo2025} decomposes rewards into per-objective advantages using a multi-headed critic and resolves gradient conflicts via PCGrad~\cite{yu2020pcgrad}. D3PO~\cite{d3po2026} preserves per-objective signals through separate policy gradient losses. MO-GRPO~\cite{mogrpo2024}, concurrent with our work, applies independent per-reward normalization to GRPO for multi-objective language-model alignment; it shares the per-component primitive with PCZ-PPO but retains GRPO's critic-free structure. CoMOPPO~\cite{kim2024comoppo} implements stage-conditioned running z-norm with multi-head critics for robotics. All require $O(K)$ value heads, scaling architectural complexity with the number of objectives.

\textbf{Domain-specific instances.}
Kortus et al.~\cite{kortus2023tracking,kortus2025constrained} apply per-component z-normalization with adapted PopArt running statistics for $K{=}2$ detector layer types in physics-domain particle tracking --- the closest domain-specific prior art (same per-component z-norm primitive). PCZ-PPO generalises the primitive to single-scalar-critic PPO+GAE at general $K$ ($K{=}2$--$8$) with batch / running-EMA statistics, framed and validated across RL and LLM alignment domains.

\textbf{Reward-shaping theory and multi-objective framing.}
Per-component z-normalization is a (non-potential-based) reward transformation; Ng, Harada and Russell~\cite{ng1999rewardshaping} established the canonical result that potential-based shaping preserves optimal policies, and more general reward transformations do so only under additional conditions. We assume user-specified priority weights $w_k$ are part of the problem specification, aligning PCZ-PPO with the preference-based scalarization scenario in Roijers et al.'s multi-objective RL taxonomy~\cite{roijers2013morl} rather than the Pareto-front-recovery scenario.

\textbf{Aggregate normalization strategies.} Aggregate-scalar approaches operate on the scalar reward or value targets without per-component decomposition: VecNormalize~\cite{raffin2021sb3} (running std of discounted returns), reward whitening~\cite{liu2023rewardwhitening,huang2024rlhf} (batch z-norm on scalar reward), advantage normalization~\cite{andrychowicz2021whatmatters,huang2022thirtysevendetails} (minibatch z-norm on $\hat{A}_t$, orthogonal to reward normalization), REINFORCE++~\cite{hu2025reinforceplusplus} (global z-norm for critic-free optimization), PopArt~\cite{hessel2019popart,hasselt2016popart} (output-layer rescaling), Return-based Scaling~\cite{schaul2021returnscaling}, and DreamerV3 symlog transforms~\cite{sullivan2023dreamerv3tricks}. Liu~\cite{liu2023rewardwhitening} argues informally that reward whitening is asymptotically redundant given a well-learned value function, but the argument assumes a single reward signal rather than a multi-component composite.

\textbf{Theoretical counter-arguments.}
Several recent works argue against per-component reward normalization on the grounds that it removes information encoded in relative reward magnitudes: GCR-PPO~\cite{gcrppo2025} motivates a multi-headed critic rather than input-side normalization for this reason, and MO-GRPO~\cite{mogrpo2024} and Constrained GRPO~\cite{girgis2026constrainedgrpo} formalise the concern by showing that scalarized-then-normalized rewards implicitly reweight by covariance structure, corrupting Lagrangian multiplier signals in constrained settings. These concerns are valid when reward magnitudes encode constraint tightness. PCZ-PPO targets environments where scales are arbitrary engineering artifacts and priorities are encoded in explicit weights $w_k$.

\textbf{Value decomposition.}
Hybrid Reward Architecture (HRA)~\cite{vanseijen2017hra} decomposes rewards into per-component Q-values in DQN, and Distributional Reward Decomposition (DRDRL)~\cite{lin2019drdrl} extends this distributionally. P3O~\cite{zhang2022p3o} uses per-stream advantage normalization for penalized objectives. Multi-GRPO~\cite{lyu2025multigrpo} implements independent per-reward normalization for text-to-image generation. LucidNFT~\cite{lucidnft2026} decouples advantage normalization for image super-resolution. These approaches either use multiple value heads ($O(K)$ overhead) or operate in critic-free settings.

\textbf{Statistical methodology for deep RL.}
Henderson et al.~\cite{henderson2018deeprl} documented the reproducibility crisis in deep RL benchmarking; Agarwal et al.~\cite{agarwal2021rliable} proposed IQM with stratified bootstrap CIs for small-$N$ studies. Our design sits in this regime; Appendix~\ref{app:stats} reports Holm--Bonferroni-corrected $p$-values, BCa-bootstrap CIs, IQM point estimates, and Vargha--Delaney $A_{12}$ stochastic-dominance scores so headline conclusions can be cross-checked under each convention.

\section{Method: PCZ-PPO}

\subsection{Reward Decomposition}

The environment reward is decomposed into $K$ independently normalizable components. The scalar reward is:
\begin{equation}
r_t = \sum_{k=1}^{K} w_k \, r_t^{(k)}
\end{equation}
where $r_t^{(k)}$ are the raw component values and $w_k$ are environment-defined importance weights. Both $r_t$ and the per-component vector $\{r_t^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K}$ are produced at each environment step via a standard \texttt{info["reward\_components"]} interface.

\subsection{Per-Component Z-Normalization}

Given a rollout buffer of $T$ timesteps across $E$ parallel environments, the component rewards form a tensor of shape $(T, E, K)$. For each component $k$, we compute statistics and normalize:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{r}_{t}^{(k)} = \frac{r_{t}^{(k)} - \mu^{(k)}}{\sigma^{(k)} + \epsilon}
\end{equation}
where $\mu^{(k)}$ and $\sigma^{(k)}$ are the mean and standard deviation of component $k$ across the buffer, and $\epsilon = 10^{-8}$. The PCZ-normalized reward is:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{r}_t = \sum_{k=1}^{K} w_k \, \tilde{r}_t^{(k)}
\end{equation}

Z-normalization equalizes \emph{scale} across components; weights $w_k$ control \emph{priority}. Without normalization, scale and priority are entangled---a high-magnitude component dominates regardless of its assigned weight.

\subsection{Running Z-Normalization Variant}

The batch z-norm (above) recomputes statistics each rollout buffer. The \textbf{running z-norm} variant smooths statistics via exponential moving averages:
\begin{equation}
\mu_{\text{run}}^{(k)} \leftarrow (1-\alpha)\,\mu_{\text{run}}^{(k)} + \alpha\,\hat{\mu}_{\text{batch}}^{(k)}, \quad
\sigma_{\text{run}}^{(k)} \leftarrow (1-\alpha)\,\sigma_{\text{run}}^{(k)} + \alpha\,\hat{\sigma}_{\text{batch}}^{(k)}
\end{equation}
with $\alpha = 0.01$. Running z-norm trades signal isolation for stationarity: the critic sees targets that change smoothly over training, reducing value function approximation error. We use the running variant (A1) as the headline configuration. We did not run a matched head-to-head between the running variant and the batch variant at the canonical configuration; the running variant was selected on theoretical grounds (smoother critic targets) and a matched ablation is in the follow-up backlog.

\subsection{GAE on Normalized Rewards}

The normalized rewards $\tilde{r}_t$ replace raw rewards in the rollout buffer. Standard GAE proceeds unchanged:
\begin{equation}
\delta_t = \tilde{r}_t + \gamma V_\phi(s_{t+1}) - V_\phi(s_t), \quad \hat{A}_t = \sum_{l=0}^{T-t-1} (\gamma\lambda)^l \delta_{t+l}
\end{equation}

Post-aggregation advantage whitening ($\hat{A} \leftarrow (\hat{A} - \mu_{\hat{A}}) / \sigma_{\hat{A}}$) bounds the gradient step regardless of aggregate reward variance. Without it (ablation A6, Table~\ref{tab:ablation}, ${\sim}93$-point gap vs A1) several seeds catastrophically collapse at $K{=}4$; whitening is a stabiliser, not a redundant safety measure.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{PCZ-PPO (Running Z-Norm Variant)}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE Initialize policy $\pi_\theta$, critic $V_\phi$, running stats $\mu_{\text{run}}^{(k)} = 0$, $\sigma_{\text{run}}^{(k)} = 1$ for $k = 1,\ldots,K$
\FOR{each iteration}
  \STATE Collect rollout buffer $\mathcal{B}$ of $T$ timesteps across $E$ environments
  \STATE Extract per-component rewards: $r_t^{(k)}$ for all $t, k$
  \STATE Update running stats: $\mu_{\text{run}}^{(k)} \leftarrow (1-\alpha)\mu_{\text{run}}^{(k)} + \alpha\,\hat{\mu}^{(k)}$, \quad $\sigma_{\text{run}}^{(k)} \leftarrow (1-\alpha)\sigma_{\text{run}}^{(k)} + \alpha\,\hat{\sigma}^{(k)}$
  \STATE Normalize: $\tilde{r}_t^{(k)} = (r_t^{(k)} - \mu_{\text{run}}^{(k)}) / (\sigma_{\text{run}}^{(k)} + \epsilon)$
  \STATE Recombine: $\tilde{r}_t = \sum_k w_k \tilde{r}_t^{(k)}$
  \STATE Compute GAE with $\tilde{r}_t$ and critic $V_\phi$ \COMMENT{Standard PPO from here}
  \STATE Whiten advantages: $\hat{A} \leftarrow (\hat{A} - \mu_{\hat{A}}) / \sigma_{\hat{A}}$
  \STATE Update $\theta, \phi$ via clipped PPO objective
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Traditional RL}

\subsubsection{Experimental Setup}

All traditional RL experiments use TorchRL~\cite{borghesi2024torchrl} with identical hyperparameters unless stated otherwise: learning rate $3 \times 10^{-4}$, $\gamma = 0.99$, $\lambda_{\text{GAE}} = 0.95$, clip $\epsilon = 0.2$, 8 PPO epochs, minibatch size 256, frames per batch 8192, 4 parallel environments, cosine entropy schedule $0.1 \to 0.01$. The primary evaluation environment is MO-LunarLander ($K{=}4$: landing, shaping, fuel\_main, fuel\_side) with component weights $[10, 5, 0.5, 0.5]$. $K{=}6$ experiments additionally restrict to the canonical weight configuration $(10, 3, 1, 1, 0.5, 0.5)$, pre-registered as the environment default before any $K{=}6$ training runs; see §\ref{app:stats} for the deduplication rule and the motivation. All standard deviations are sample std (ddof${}=1$) across $N$ seeds.\footnote{We use sample standard deviation $s = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N-1}\sum_i(x_i - \bar{x})^2}$ (ddof${}=1$) following Agarwal et al.~\cite{agarwal2021rliable}.}

\subsubsection{K-Scaling: The Central Result}

\textbf{Pre-registered hypothesis (partially falsified).} We pre-registered the hypothesis that PCZ-PPO's advantage over PPO grows monotonically with $K$ because aggregate normalization loses more information as the number of components increases. The data falsifies the monotonicity but supports a weaker stability-preservation claim: PCZ-PPO's mean is approximately invariant across $K$ at the canonical schedule for each $K$, while PPO degrades unpredictably. The mean PCZ$-$PPO gap is non-monotonic ($\cnum{k4_delta} \to \cnum{k6_delta} \to \cnum{k8_delta}$) and the K=2 cell is a negative control where PPO wins.

We test this using LunarLander wrappers at $K{=}2$ (coarse: landing + dense aggregate, weights $(10,6)$), $K{=}4$ (native decomposition, weights $(10,5,0.5,0.5)$), $K{=}6$ (intermediate split, weights $(10,3,1,1,0.5,0.5)$), and $K{=}8$ (shaping split into 5 sub-potentials, equal weights). \textbf{K is confounded with weight heterogeneity:} max/min weight ratio is roughly $1.7\!:\!1$ at $K{=}2$, $20\!:\!1$ at $K{=}4$, $20\!:\!1$ at $K{=}6$, and $1\!:\!1$ at $K{=}8$. The K-scaling result therefore mixes two effects --- decomposition granularity and weight heterogeneity --- and we report it as such, with the weight-sensitivity table (§\ref{sec:weight_sensitivity}, Tables~\ref{tab:weight_sensitivity}--\ref{tab:weight_sensitivity_k8}) providing the orthogonal weight-only sweep.

The honest reading is a \emph{stability-preservation} pattern, not a monotone performance gain. PCZ-PPO's mean is approximately flat across $K{\in}\{4,6,8\}$ ($\cnum{k4_pcz_stat}$, $\cnum{k6_pcz_stat}$, $\cnum{k8_pcz_stat}$; range ${\approx}20$ points), while PPO is unstable ($\cnum{k4_ppo_stat} \to \cnum{k6_ppo_stat} \to \cnum{k8_ppo_stat}$, range ${>}100$ points; Figure~\ref{fig:kscaling}). \textbf{Multi-head PPO helps but is insufficient.} Multi-head PPO v2 (per-component value heads with supervision, C5) achieves $\cnum{ablC5_stat}$ ($n{=}\cnum{ablC5_seeds}$) --- better than PPO ($\cnum{ablB1_mean}$, $n{=}\cnum{ablB1_seeds}$) but below PCZ-PPO ($\cnum{ablA1_mean}$, $n{=}\cnum{ablA1_seeds}$). Decomposing the critic captures partial benefit; per-component reward normalization is more effective and requires no architectural changes. The $n{=}10$ replication widened the A1--C5 gap (one new C5 seed s51 crashed, inflating SD from $16.2$ at $n{=}3$ to $54.8$ at $n{=}10$); Welch on A1 vs C5 at matched $n{=}10$ gives $p{=}0.023$, $d{=}1.15$. Cross-$K$ confirmation at $K{=}6$: $\cnum{k6_mh_stat}$ ($n{=}\cnum{k6_mh_seeds}$) below both PPO ($\cnum{k6_ppo_stat}$, $n{=}\cnum{k6_ppo_seeds}$) and PCZ-PPO ($\cnum{k6_pcz_stat}$, $n{=}\cnum{k6_pcz_seeds}$); multi-head supervision degrades faster than standard PPO as $K$ grows.

\textbf{Output-side normalization fails.} PopArt-PPO ($\cnum{ablPopArt_mean}$) is the worst performer---rescaling value head outputs does not address the variance hijacking problem at the reward level. Combining PopArt with PCZ (PCZ-PPO + PopArt, $\cnum{ablGDPOPopArt_mean}$) is also destructive, confirming that input-side per-component z-norm and output-side adaptive rescaling are incompatible at the same time.

\textbf{Weights are critical after z-normalization.} Equal weights after z-norm yield $\cnum{abl_eqw_stat}$ ($n{=}\cnum{abl_eqw_seeds}$, weights $(1,1,1,1)$) vs.\ $\cnum{ablA1_stat}$ ($n{=}\cnum{ablA1_seeds}$) with primary weights $(10,5,0.5,0.5)$, a delta of $\cnum{abl_eqw_delta}$ points. Z-normalization removes natural scale priority, making explicit weights the \emph{only} mechanism for expressing component importance.

\subsection{Scope-Boundary Investigations and Supporting Analyses}
\label{sec:scope_boundary}

Four supporting analyses (full details in App.~\ref{app:scope_boundary}):

\textbf{Compute-matched comparison against GRPO} (App.~\ref{app:gym_grpo_compute_matched}). GRPO~\cite{shao2024grpo} at $2{\times}$ timestep budget fails on all four sequential MDPs we tested (LunarLander $K{\in}\{4,8\}$, BipedalWalker $K{=}3$, HalfCheetah $K{=}2$). The critic's role is temporal credit assignment, not reward normalization --- GRPO fails even on dense same-scale rewards (HalfCheetah). The D1 ablation cell (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}) closes the gym $2{\times}2$ factorial: removing the critic from PCZ-PPO collapses A1's $\cnum{ablA1_stat}$ to $\cnum{ablD1_stat}$.

\textbf{Computational cost} (App.~\ref{app:gym_compute_cost}). The z-norm preprocessing is $O(TK)$ per batch; measured wall-clock overhead is $-2$\% to $+5$\% across CartPole, LunarLander, HalfCheetah, and BipedalWalker. No extra parameters; a single scalar critic suffices.

\textbf{Synthetic trading: directional PCZ advantage in the homogeneous-scale variant, underperformance in the heterogeneous-scale variant} (App.~\ref{app:gym_trading}, Figure~\ref{fig:trading}). On a synthetic Ornstein--Uhlenbeck mean-reversion environment, the \emph{homogeneous-scale} variant (Figure~\ref{fig:trading}) shows PCZ-PPO directionally above PPO at every $K{\in}\{2,4,6,8\}$ at the 5M competence horizon, but no comparison reaches statistical significance: $K{=}4$ at $n{=}\cnum{tv172_n}$ ($\Delta{=}\cnum{tv172_delta}$, $p{=}\cnum{tv172_welch_p}$); $K{\in}\{2,6,8\}$ at $n{=}\cnum{tv173_n}$ (all $p{>}0.05$, with $K{\in}\{6,8\}$ showing underpowered $d{\approx}0.73$ trends). A Stage-1 1M HP sweep produced a low-$n$ directional PCZ signal that did not clear $\alpha{=}0.05$ at the powered Stage-2 5M replication. A \emph{separate} heterogeneous-scale variant (which adds terminal-bankruptcy events at $-1000$ on top of per-step P\&L $O(1)$, tested at 100k/500k only, \emph{not in Figure~\ref{fig:trading}}) shows the opposite direction: PCZ underperforms PPO. The mechanism is event sparsity (bankruptcy fires at most once per episode), not scale heterogeneity. Together these establish a second failure mode complementary to the weight-sensitivity cliff (§\ref{sec:weight_sensitivity}): PCZ requires reward components to fire \emph{densely}; terminal-event or low-frequency components fail regardless of scale magnitude.

\textbf{LLM alignment is a clean null across four regimes} (App.~\ref{app:llm}). Single-step preference alignment on Qwen2.5-0.5B + 4-bit QLoRA across four reward-stack regimes ($K{\in}\{4,6\}$, PPO and RLOO~\cite{ahmadian2024rloo}, 200--1000 steps) shows no PCZ benefit: the only short-horizon variance-reduction signature ($K{=}6$/200 steps) reverses by 500 steps. The $2{\times}2$ factorial $\{$PPO, RLOO$\}{\times}\{$Standard, PCZ$\}$ shows the critic main effect dominates and PCZ is null in both critic and critic-free settings. The per-component mechanism does not transfer to single-step preference alignment with this reward-stack shape and model scale; multi-turn or process-reward settings remain open (§\ref{sec:future_work}).

These four investigations confirm the \emph{regime of validity} of the headline claim: PCZ-PPO is useful for sequential MDPs with heterogeneous-scale densely-firing components and strictly-positive heterogeneous weights; outside that envelope the contribution is null.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{When PCZ-PPO Helps and When It Does Not}

Our results identify three conditions for PCZ-PPO to help, all of which must be met:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Heterogeneous component scales} (sparse/dense mismatch or order-of-magnitude scale differences). HalfCheetah and Reacher (homogeneous components) show parity; LunarLander and BipedalWalker (sparse-terminal + dense) show a gap.
\item \textbf{Densely-firing components.} Every reward component must fire at every step or near-every step. Terminal-event or low-frequency components (e.g.\ bankruptcy in heterogeneous-trading, §\ref{sec:trading}) destabilise the running-z-norm estimate when they first fire and PCZ-PPO underperforms PPO --- even though the heterogeneous-trading scale spread is three orders of magnitude.
\item \textbf{Strictly-positive heterogeneous weights.} Equal weights, flat weights, and any zero-weighted-component schedule cause PCZ-PPO to underperform PPO at $K{\geq}4$/long horizons (§\ref{sec:weight_sensitivity}).
\end{enumerate}

We conjecture PCZ-PPO is most useful in complex environments with many densely-firing reward terms with heterogeneous scales (robotics with 10+ shaping terms, RLHF with multiple dense reward models); this is untested outside LunarLander wrappers and labelled as future work.

\textbf{Catastrophic-failure reduction.} On sparse/dense-mixed environments at plateau horizon (4M steps, $n{=}\cnum{cfail_bw_n}$ seeds each), PCZ-PPO records zero catastrophic failures (eval mean ${<}0$) where PPO incurs them regularly. \subsection{Weight Sensitivity: Precision vs.\ Robustness}
\label{sec:weight_sensitivity}

The counterintuitive corollary: the naïvely-safe ``all-equal'' default is the \emph{worst} choice for PCZ, whereas PPO retains a healthy mean under uniform weights because raw-scale priority is already encoded in the environment's component magnitudes. Off-canonical rows use $n{=}5$ at seeds 42--46; the canonical row reuses the headline $n{=}\cnum{k4_pcz_seeds}$ sample. \subsection{Hyperparameter and Entropy Sensitivity (at 500k)}
\label{sec:hp_fairness}
\label{sec:entropy_coupling}

PCZ's canonical-cosine advantage is not a hyperparameter artifact, and PCZ tolerates a wider entropy ceiling than PPO. Three complementary 500k sweeps establish this; the 4M peak-vs-peak comparison is canonical-only and discussed as a limitation in §\ref{sec:limitations}.

\textbf{Targeted PPO tuning} (Figure~\ref{fig:hp_fairness}; 5 entropies $\times$ 3 LRs $\times$ 2 seeds at $K{=}4$/500k canonical weights). PPO's peak cell ($\mathrm{lr}{=}3\mathrm{e}{-}4$, $\mathrm{ent}{=}0.0$) reaches $\cnum{w7_ppo_best_stat}$ ($n{=}\cnum{w7_ppo_best_seeds}$); rerunning PCZ at the same cell gives $\cnum{w7_pcz_best_stat}$ ($n{=}\cnum{w7_pcz_best_seeds}$). The canonical-cosine gap $\cnum{k4_delta}$ narrows to a peak-vs-peak gap of $\cnum{w7_delta_best}$ --- partly a tuning artifact, partly algorithmic.

\textbf{Full per-algo grid} (Figure~\ref{fig:hp_peak}, Table~\ref{tab:hp_sweep_full}; 3 LRs $\times$ 3 entropy configs, $n{=}3$ per cell). At canonical heterogeneous weights, the algorithms are matched ($\cnum{hp19_k4_heterog_pcz_best_stat}$ vs $\cnum{hp19_k4_heterog_ppo_best_stat}$). At flat $(3,3,3,3)$ and equal $(1,1,1,1)$ weights, PCZ collapses by ${\sim}200$ points regardless of HP, consistent with the weight-sensitivity cliff (§\ref{sec:weight_sensitivity}).

\textbf{Entropy ceiling} (Figure~\ref{fig:entropy_sweep}, Table~\ref{tab:entropy_sweep}; fixed $\mathrm{ent}\in\{0,0.01,0.03,0.05,0.10\}$, $n{=}5$ per cell). PCZ-PPO stays healthy up to $\mathrm{ent}{=}0.05$; PPO degrades at $\mathrm{ent}{=}0.03$ and fully collapses at $\mathrm{ent}{=}0.05$ (largest PCZ advantage at $\mathrm{ent}{=}0.05$, $\Delta{=}\cnum{rr6_delta_e05}$). Per-component z-norm bounds gradient variance, so higher exploration does not destabilise learning --- PCZ's $5{\times}$ wider entropy ceiling explains its lower sensitivity to entropy-schedule choice in the canonical comparison.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\textbf{Single strong positive environment.} LunarLander (and its K-variants) is the primary positive result in traditional RL. The cross-environment analysis (§\ref{sec:trading}) yields a clean null on synthetic trading (both homogeneous- and heterogeneous-scale variants), and HalfCheetah at 500k yields a null with a known undertraining caveat. A Humanoid pilot ($K{=}3$, 2M, $n{=}\cnum{humanoid_n}$ each, cosine entropy 0.1$\to$0.01) returned $0/\cnum{humanoid_n}$ catastrophic failures in \emph{both} algorithms, so the deployment-reliability gap (Table~\ref{tab:failure_rate}) does not appear; on mean reward PPO outperforms PCZ ($\cnum{humanoid_ppo_stat}$ vs $\cnum{humanoid_pcz_stat}$, $\Delta{=}\cnum{humanoid_delta}$). Humanoid is therefore not a third positive-PCZ environment; the failure-rate claim is bounded to environments where PPO catastrophic failures structurally occur under canonical hyperparameters. The qualitative pattern is that PCZ-PPO requires both (a) heterogeneous reward-component scales \emph{and} (b) dense per-step component activation; environments missing either condition do not show a benefit. Additional environments combining both conditions --- particularly in LLM alignment, where GDPO originated --- would strengthen the claims (Section~\ref{sec:llm_alignment}).

\textbf{Weight specification requirement.} PCZ-PPO requires the user to specify meaningful weights. Adaptive weight learning (e.g., GradNorm) could remove this dependency and is a natural follow-up direction.

\textbf{Non-stationary critic targets.} The running z-norm variant mitigates but does not eliminate non-stationarity in the critic's targets. The critic loss under PCZ-PPO may be persistently higher than under VecNormalize. Formal convergence analysis remains an open question.

\textbf{Entropy-configuration asymmetry on BipedalWalker and HalfCheetah catastrophic-failure rates.} The Table~\ref{tab:failure_rate} headline compares mixed-configuration PPO seeds (5 cosine 0.1$\to$0.01 + 5 fixed $\mathrm{ent\_coef}{=}0.0$) against mixed-configuration PCZ-PPO seeds (5 cosine + 5 fixed $\mathrm{ent\_coef}{=}0.01$). Per-seed inspection shows all PPO failures concentrate in the $\mathrm{ent\_coef}{=}0.0$ subgroup (3/5 each on BW and HC; 0/5 in the cosine subgroup); PCZ-PPO was not tested at $\mathrm{ent\_coef}{=}0.0$. The matched-cosine Humanoid 2M comparison (above) shows $0/10$ failures in both algorithms, consistent with the asymmetry hypothesis. We ran this balanced rerun (TV1.8): PCZ-PPO at $\mathrm{ent\_coef}{=}0.0$, $n{=}\cnum{cfail_bw_zeroent_n}$ seeds per env, same lr and weights as the original runs. BipedalWalker: PCZ \cnum{cfail_bw_zeroent_pcz_fail_n}/\cnum{cfail_bw_zeroent_n} failures ($\cnum{cfail_bw_zeroent_pcz_pct}$\%) vs PPO \cnum{cfail_bw_zeroent_ppo_fail_n}/\cnum{cfail_bw_zeroent_n} ($\cnum{cfail_bw_zeroent_ppo_pct}$\%). HalfCheetah: PCZ \cnum{cfail_hc_zeroent_pcz_fail_n}/\cnum{cfail_hc_zeroent_n} ($\cnum{cfail_hc_zeroent_pcz_pct}$\%) vs PPO \cnum{cfail_hc_zeroent_ppo_fail_n}/\cnum{cfail_hc_zeroent_n} ($\cnum{cfail_hc_zeroent_ppo_pct}$\%). Both algorithms fail at zero entropy on both environments. The headline deployment-reliability difference originates from the entropy-configuration asymmetry in the original mixed-config comparison, not from an algorithmic property of PCZ-PPO. Full per-seed breakdown in Table~\ref{tab:entropy_subgroup} (Appendix~\ref{app:entropy_subgroup}).

\textbf{LunarLander-only K-scaling.} K-scaling uses wrappers of the same underlying environment. We attempted a second environment family on HalfCheetah ($K{\in}\{2,4,6,8\}$, 5 seeds each, 500k): null at $K{\in}\{2,4\}$ ($\cnum{ceHC_k4_delta}$, $\cnum{ceHC_k4_var_ratio}\times$ variance reduction), PCZ underperforms by $\cnum{ceHC_k8_delta}$ at $K{=}8$ ($\cnum{ceHC_k8_pcz_stat}$ vs $\cnum{ceHC_k8_ppo_stat}$). Mechanism: every HalfCheetah component has CV in $[0.75, 1.92]$ (homogeneous-scale), versus LunarLander-K8 velocity at CV${\approx}177$; the noise-amplification mechanism cannot generalize to homogeneous-CV envs. Caveat: 500k HC is below the ${\sim}2$M-step competence threshold (undertrained-null, not plateau-null). Longer-horizon HC ($\geq$2M) and synthetic trading (§\ref{sec:trading}) round out the cross-env nulls.

\textbf{Peak-vs-peak HP fairness verified at 500k only.} The hyperparameter-fairness audit (§\ref{sec:hp_fairness}) and the entropy-coupling sweep (§\ref{sec:entropy_coupling}) are conducted at $K{=}4$/500k. At 4M, all comparisons in this paper are canonical-vs-canonical (lr$=3{\times}10^{-4}$, cosine 0.1$\to$0.01) --- no HP sweep has been run at 4M for either algorithm. This means: (a) the K=4/4M $\Delta{=}\cnum{w10_k4_4M_delta}$ in PCZ's favour is canonical-canonical, not peak-vs-peak; (b) the K=8/4M weight-sensitivity cliff (Figure~\ref{fig:weight_sensitivity}) is canonical HPs at each weight tier --- whether a different HP cell (longer entropy schedule, higher LR) would lift PCZ-PPO above PPO under flat or equal weights is open. The peak-vs-peak algorithmic-merit claim is therefore established at the 500k horizon; at 4M it remains an inference from canonical-config evidence only. A 4M peak-vs-peak HP grid (~115 CPU-h per K) is the natural follow-up but was deferred for compute reasons.

\textbf{Plateau behaviour and the sample-efficiency framing.} Trained to 4M with matched TorchRL infrastructure on three envs (Figure~\ref{fig:sample_efficiency}, Figure~\ref{fig:learning_curves_4M}): on LunarLander $K{=}4$ at $n{=}\cnum{w10_k4_4M_pcz_seeds}$, PCZ retains a directional advantage ($\cnum{w10_k4_pcz_stat}$ vs $\cnum{w10_k4_ppo_stat}$, $\Delta{=}\cnum{w10_k4_4M_delta}$, PPO/PCZ variance $\cnum{w10_k4_4M_var_ratio}{\times}$); on BipedalWalker $K{=}3$ at $n{=}\cnum{w10_bw_4M_pcz_seeds}$, PCZ ($\cnum{w10_bw_pcz_stat}$) beats PPO ($\cnum{w10_bw_ppo_stat}$, $\Delta{=}\cnum{w10_bw_4M_delta}$, variance ratio $\cnum{w10_bw_4M_var_ratio}{\times}$) and \emph{three of ten} PPO seeds collapse to an $\approx{-}92$ floor while all ten PCZ seeds learn --- a 30\% catastrophic-failure rate the earlier $n{=}5$ read missed; on HalfCheetah $K{=}2$ at $n{=}\cnum{w10_hc_4M_pcz_seeds}$, PCZ ($\cnum{w10_hc_pcz_stat}$) vs PPO ($\cnum{w10_hc_ppo_stat}$, $\Delta{=}\cnum{w10_hc_4M_delta}$, variance ratio $\cnum{w10_hc_4M_var_ratio}{\times}$) is driven by shared seed-specific instability (s48 collapses for both, s49/s51 only for PPO). The earlier $n{=}3$ ``converge to the same asymptote'' reading was an under-sampled artifact. At $n{=}10$, PCZ-PPO retains \emph{both} a sample-efficiency advantage (the gap narrows from $\cnum{k4_delta}$ at 500k to $\cnum{w10_k4_4M_delta}$ at 4M for $K{=}4$, but does not vanish) \emph{and} a plateau-mean advantage on all three sparse/dense-mixed envs ($\Delta{\geq}{+}29$ on mean, ${\geq}2.1{\times}$ variance reduction in every cell). The 4M data therefore \emph{contradicts} a strong reading of Liu's~\cite{liu2023rewardwhitening} informal argument that reward whitening becomes asymptotically redundant when the value function is well-trained: the gap narrows but is not eliminated at the horizons we tested. Whether the gap continues to close at horizons we did not test ($\geq$10M, the true asymptote) is open.

\textbf{$K{=}8$ at 4M requires hand-designed heterogeneous weights.} Three weight regimes (Figure~\ref{fig:learning_curves_4M}, Table~\ref{tab:weight_sensitivity_k8}): \textbf{(a) equal} (env default, all 1.0) --- PCZ collapses to $\cnum{rr16_equal_k8_4M_pcz_stat}$ vs PPO $\cnum{rr16_equal_k8_4M_ppo_stat}$ ($n{=}\cnum{rr16_equal_k8_4M_pcz_seeds}$, $\Delta{=}\cnum{rr16_equal_k8_4M_delta}$); \textbf{(b) zero-component "SNR-informed"} $(10,5,0,1,0.5,0.5,0.5,0.5)$ that zeros the noisiest channel (velocity $\sigma/|\mu|{\approx}177$) --- PCZ collapses identically ($\cnum{rr16h8_k8_4M_pcz_stat}$ vs $\cnum{rr16h8_k8_4M_ppo_stat}$, $n{=}3$, $\Delta{=}\cnum{rr16h8_k8_4M_delta}$); \textbf{(c) heterogeneous strictly-positive} $(10,1,1,1,1,1,0.5,0.5)$ --- PCZ wins ($\cnum{w10_k8_pcz_stat}$ vs $\cnum{w10_k8_ppo_stat}$, $n{=}\cnum{rr16h7_k8_4M_pcz_seeds}$/$\cnum{rr16h7_k8_4M_ppo_seeds}$, $\Delta{=}\cnum{rr16h7_k8_4M_delta}$, $\cnum{rr16h7_k8_4M_var_ratio}{\times}$ lower variance). Cohen's $d$ runs monotonically in heterogeneity ($\cnum{rr16h7_k8_4M_cohen_d}$ H7 $\to$ $\cnum{rr16_moderate_k8_4M_cohen_d}$ moderate $\to$ $\cnum{rr16_flat_k8_4M_cohen_d}$ flat $\to$ $\cnum{rr16_equal_k8_4M_cohen_d}$ equal), replicating the $K{=}4$ cliff (Table~\ref{tab:weight_sensitivity}) at longer horizon. \textbf{Why zero-weighted channels break PCZ:} the channel still participates in per-component variance accounting but contributes nothing to the scalar reward, leaving a degenerate normalisation step that injects noise into GAE without compensating signal; PPO is robust because it sees only the post-weighting scalar. \textbf{Usage constraint:} every declared component must carry a strictly positive weight; to remove a component, omit it from \texttt{info["reward\_components"]} rather than weight it to zero. This forecloses naïve auto-SNR schemes that drive any weight below a positive floor.

\subsection{Future Work}
\label{sec:future_work}

\textbf{Adaptive weight learning.} PCZ-PPO's performance is sensitive to the choice of component weights $w_k$ (swing of $\cnum{abl_eqw_delta}$ points between optimal and equal weights at $K{=}4$/500k). This is the method's most significant practical limitation. Future work will investigate automatic weight discovery methods---GradNorm~\cite{chen2018gradnorm}, Dynamic Weight Averaging, and uncertainty-based weighting---applied on top of PCZ-PPO's per-component normalization. The combination of automatic normalization (PCZ) with automatic priority discovery (adaptive weights) would eliminate the need for manual weight specification entirely.

\textbf{LLM alignment validation.} PCZ-PPO's per-component normalization is directly applicable to RLHF settings where multiple reward models (helpfulness, safety, format compliance) produce heterogeneous signals~\cite{deepseek2025r1}. Our single-step preference-alignment runs (§\ref{sec:llm_alignment}) are null; multi-turn dialogue or process-reward (per-step) settings, where the critic+GAE pipeline is more naturally aligned with the gym-RL findings, remain open. Validating K-scaling on multi-turn RLHF with $K{=}2{-}8$ reward models would connect to GDPO's original domain and test cross-domain generality.

\textbf{Hardened factorial and ablation replications.} The D1 cell (PCZ-PPO without critic) and the A6 cell (advantage whitening off) are at $n{=}5$ and $n{=}10$ respectively; the qualitative directions (D1 collapses, A6 destabilises) are unambiguous, but a hardened replication at $n{\geq}10$ for D1 and a higher-$n$ extension for A6 would tighten the factorial conclusion that "the critic absorbs PCZ-introduced variance" and the ablation conclusion that "advantage whitening is a stabiliser, not a redundancy."

\textbf{$K{=}8$/4M hyperparameter-grid search.} Whether a full HP-grid search at $K{=}8$/4M ($\sim$115 CPU-hours) would lift PCZ-PPO above PPO under non-heterogeneous weight schedules is open; the canonical-cosine results in Table~\ref{tab:bestofbest} all show collapse, but we have not searched the (LR $\times$ entropy-schedule) plane at this horizon.

\textbf{Cross-environment K-scaling.} The high-$K$ generalisation conjecture is unsupported outside LunarLander wrappers (HalfCheetah at 500k is undertrained-null and synthetic trading is null-to-negative, §\ref{sec:trading}); a longer-horizon HalfCheetah K=4/6/8 sweep ($\geq$2M steps) and a second sparse/dense-mixed environment family would either confirm or close the K-scaling story.

\textbf{Formal convergence analysis.} The running z-norm variant mitigates but does not eliminate non-stationarity in the critic's value targets. A formal analysis of convergence guarantees under per-component normalization with a single critic remains open.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented PCZ-PPO, a method that bridges per-component reward normalization from critic-free GRPO settings to standard PPO with a single scalar critic. It adds no neural-architecture changes (a small reward-preprocessing wrapper around PPO+GAE, ${\sim}70$ lines, Appendix~\ref{app:repro}); it requires the environment to expose per-component rewards via \texttt{info["reward\_components"]} and the user to specify weights $w_k$. The contribution is bounded: in compute-constrained regimes with heterogeneous reward scales \emph{and} densely-firing components \emph{and} hand-designed strictly-positive heterogeneous weights, PCZ delivers deployment reliability and a sample-efficiency benefit; outside that envelope (homogeneous-scale envs, sparse-event components, equal/flat/zero-weighted schedules, long horizons) it matches PPO at best and underperforms at worst. Synthetic trading (§\ref{sec:trading}) is null at $K{=}4$/5M ($n{=}\cnum{tv172_n}$, $p{=}\cnum{tv172_welch_p}$); $K{\in}\{2,6,8\}$ at 5M ($n{=}\cnum{tv173_n}$) confirm the null at $\alpha{=}0.05$, with an underpowered $d{\approx}0.73$ PCZ trend at $K{\in}\{6,8\}$ flagged for follow-up. LLM alignment (§\ref{sec:llm_alignment}) is a clean null across all four regimes.

Four findings structure the contribution. (1) \emph{Deployment reliability.} At plateau horizon (4M steps, $n{=}\cnum{cfail_bw_n}$), PCZ-PPO records zero catastrophic failures on sparse/dense-mixed environments where PPO incurs them regularly: BipedalWalker $K{=}3$: $\cnum{cfail_bw_pcz_pct}$\% vs $\cnum{cfail_bw_ppo_pct}$\% failure rate; HalfCheetah $K{=}2$: $\cnum{cfail_hc_pcz_pct}$\%$\dagger$ vs $\cnum{cfail_hc_ppo_pct}$\% ($\dagger$one shared-instability seed; HP-robustness-envelope reading discussed in §\ref{sec:limitations}). (2) \emph{Stability-preserving K-scaling at 500k.} PCZ's mean stays in a ${\approx}20$-point band across $K{\in}\{4,6,8\}$ ($\cnum{k4_pcz_stat}$, $\cnum{k6_pcz_stat}$, $\cnum{k8_pcz_stat}$) while PPO swings ${>}100$ points ($\cnum{k4_ppo_stat}$, $\cnum{k6_ppo_stat}$, $\cnum{k8_ppo_stat}$); the gap ($\cnum{k4_delta}$, $\cnum{k6_delta}$, $\cnum{k8_delta}$) is non-monotonic and widens at $K{=}8$. Under Holm--Bonferroni at $n{=}\cnum{k4_pcz_seeds},\cnum{k6_pcz_seeds},\cnum{k8_pcz_seeds}$ all three $K$-values survive at $\alpha{=}0.05$ (Holm $\cnum{k4_holm_p}$, $\cnum{k6_holm_p}$, $\cnum{k8_holm_p}$; permutation $\cnum{k8_perm_p}$ and Mann--Whitney $\cnum{k8_mwu_p}$ also reject at $K{=}8$); the headline reads as stability-preservation (PCZ variance at $K{=}8$ is $\cnum{k8_var_ratio}{\times}$ lower; BCa CIs exclude zero at $K{\in}\{4,8\}$, barely at $K{=}6$ $\cnum{k6_bca_ci}$). (3) \emph{Partial asymptotic convergence with persistent variance advantage at 4M.} On LunarLander $K{=}4$/4M at $n{=}\cnum{w10_k4_4M_pcz_seeds}$, PCZ ($\cnum{w10_k4_pcz_stat}$) retains a directional advantage over PPO ($\cnum{w10_k4_ppo_stat}$, $\Delta{=}\cnum{w10_k4_4M_delta}$, variance ratio $\cnum{w10_k4_4M_var_ratio}{\times}$); PPO narrows but does not close the 500k gap by 4M. (4) \emph{Critic$\times$normalization interaction in gym, not in single-step LLM alignment.} The gym $2{\times}2$ factorial (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}) shows PCZ helps with critic (A1 $\cnum{ablA1_stat}$) but collapses without it (D1 $\cnum{ablD1_stat}$, ${\sim}400$-point drop); the LLM $2{\times}2$ factorial (§\ref{sec:llm_alignment}, regime 4) does not replicate the interaction --- PCZ is null in both critic and critic-free single-step preference alignment.

\emph{Weight-selection requirement at high $K$/long horizons (§\ref{sec:limitations}).} At $K{=}8$/4M under env-default equal weights, PCZ ($\cnum{rr16_equal_k8_4M_pcz_stat}$, $n{=}\cnum{rr16_equal_k8_4M_pcz_seeds}$) underperforms PPO ($\cnum{rr16_equal_k8_4M_ppo_stat}$, $\Delta{=}\cnum{rr16_equal_k8_4M_delta}$); zero-weighted-component schedules reproduce the collapse ($\cnum{rr16h8_k8_4M_pcz_stat}$); only hand-designed heterogeneous strictly-positive $(10,1,1,1,1,1,0.5,0.5)$ lets PCZ beat PPO ($\cnum{w10_k8_pcz_stat}$ vs $\cnum{w10_k8_ppo_stat}$, $\Delta{=}\cnum{rr16h7_k8_4M_delta}$, $\cnum{rr16h7_k8_4M_var_ratio}{\times}$ lower variance, $n{=}\cnum{rr16h7_k8_4M_pcz_seeds}$). PCZ-PPO is a careful-weights replacement for PPO in compute-constrained $K{\geq}4$ regimes with heterogeneously-weighted densely-firing components, not a drop-in replacement. The algorithmic delta is a short reward-preprocessing wrapper, not a new architecture; the value is in the systematic when-it-helps / when-it-doesn't analysis.

\textbf{Why PCZ-PPO helps:} The \texttt{landing} bonus ($\pm 100$) has $\sim$3000$\times$ the magnitude of \texttt{fuel\_side} ($\pm 0.03$). \textbf{K=2 behaviour:} With only 2 components (\texttt{landing} $\pm100$ + \texttt{dense} aggregate), PCZ-PPO does \emph{not} outperform PPO --- PPO scores higher ($\cnum{k2_ppo_stat}$ vs $\cnum{k2_pcz_stat}$, $d{=}\cnum{k2_welch_d}$, Welch $p_{\text{raw}}{=}\cnum{k2_welch_p}$, $n{=}\cnum{k2_pcz_seeds}$). K=2 acts as a \emph{negative control}: coarser component granularity leaves less room for variance hijacking, consistent with the mechanism of §\ref{sec:variance_hijacking}. K=2 is a 5-seed supporting-evidence cell and is not part of the Holm family.

\subsection{Reproducibility \& Statistical Methodology}
\label{app:stats}

\textbf{Seed selection.} The pre-registered headline design is seeds 42--51 (10 seeds) per $K{\in}\{4,6,8\}$ and 42--46 (5 seeds) for $K{=}2$, plus 3--5 seeds for supporting-evidence cells. Seeds are chosen sequentially from seed 42 with no outcome-dependent filtering. $K{\in}\{4,6\}$ were extended with five robustness seeds (52--56) after the pre-registered sweep completed, and $K{\in}\{4,6,8\}$ were subsequently extended with ten Holm-extension seeds (57--66) (final counts in Table~\ref{tab:kscaling}). The two analyses have the same sign of effect but different significance verdicts: the pre-registered $n{=}10$ ($K{=}6$ Welch $p{=}0.0017$, Holm $p{=}0.005$, PCZ $+167.2{\pm}35.1$ vs PPO $+110.3{\pm}34.1$) reaches significance at $\alpha{=}0.05$, while the extended $n{=}\cnum{k6_pcz_seeds}$ ($K{=}6$ Welch $p{=}\cnum{k6_welch_p}$, Holm $p{=}\cnum{k6_holm_p}$, Table~\ref{tab:kscaling}) does not. The seed extensions raised the PPO mean and shrank the gap. Both are reported transparently. All rendered fragments use the full $n{=}\cnum{k6_pcz_seeds}$ dataset to maximise statistical power; the $n{=}10$ subset statistics are provided in this paragraph for reader replication of the pre-registered analysis.

\textbf{Run-level deduplication rule (explicit).} Multiple runs exist in the data repository for some \emph{(algorithm, environment, timesteps, seed)} tuples (infrastructure changes --- GAE code fixes, weight-metadata backfills, TorchRL version bumps --- required re-running seeds for fairness). We resolve to one row per unique seed using:
\begin{quote}
\emph{Keep the chronologically-latest run per unique seed.}
\end{quote}
This rule is fixed in the data-query layer of the public code release (Appendix~\ref{app:repro}) and does not depend on outcome. Two secondary filters isolate the canonical configuration: (i) \emph{component-weight filter} at $K{=}4$ ($(10,5,0.5,0.5)$) and $K{=}6$ ($(10,3,1,1,0.5,0.5)$), the env defaults; (ii) \emph{entropy-schedule filter} \texttt{ent\_coef\_schedule="0.1:0.01"} at $K{\in}\{2,4,6,8\}$ to isolate canonical cosine-schedule runs from fixed-entropy tuning-audit runs that re-use seeds 42--46. The $K{=}6$ weight filter is necessary because the database contains an accidental seed-42 run with an empty \texttt{component\_weights} column (the training command omitted the flag and the env-default loaded at runtime); under chrono-latest dedupe alone this row would silently displace the canonical seed-42 row. The accidental row is identified post-hoc by its empty column, not by outcome. A prior revision of this paper reported the pre-filter numbers; full forensic detail (pre-fix vs post-fix means, SDs, and Welch $p$-values) is in the repo errata. General lesson: under chrono-latest dedupe, any off-config exploratory run landing on a headline seed will silently displace the canonical row unless the pipeline filters by config explicitly.

\textbf{Reacher-variant exclusion.} Reacher data contains four runs per algorithm (three at 100k steps, one at 5k steps). The 5k-step runs are pre-convergence signal checks and are excluded from all reported Reacher aggregates; the 100k runs are used. This is the only case where we exclude runs by timestep budget rather than by the chronologically-latest rule above.

\textbf{Weight-configuration subset.} LunarLander $K{=}4$ filters to the primary weight configuration $(10,5,0.5,0.5)$ and $K{=}6$ to the canonical $(10,3,1,1,0.5,0.5)$, both pre-registered to match the component-scale hierarchy in Table~\ref{tab:app_comp_stats}. The $K{=}6$ filter additionally excludes one accidentally-launched seed-42 run with empty weights column (see dedupe rule above). Off-primary $K{=}4$ exploratory sweeps at $(1,1,1,1)$ or $(0.5,0.5,5,5)$ are excluded from aggregates (not outcome-based). $K{=}2$ and $K{=}8$ each use a single canonical config.

\textbf{Statistical rigor tiers.} Headline claims (abstract, title, K-scaling table for $K{\geq}4$) use $\cnum{k4_pcz_seeds}$ seeds for $K{\in}\{4,6\}$, $\cnum{k8_pcz_seeds}$ seeds for $K{=}8$, with bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals and Welch's $t$-tests. Supporting evidence (cross-environment, ablation components) uses 3--5 seeds. Uncorrected Welch's $t$-test $p$-values for the three simultaneous headline comparisons are $K{=}4$ ($p{=}\cnum{k4_welch_p}$), $K{=}6$ ($p{=}\cnum{k6_welch_p}$), $K{=}8$ ($p{=}\cnum{k8_welch_p}$). Exact two-sided permutation tests (20\,000 resamples, fixed seed 20260416) give $K{=}4$ $p_{\text{perm}}{=}\cnum{k4_perm_p}$, $K{=}6$ $p_{\text{perm}}{=}\cnum{k6_perm_p}$, $K{=}8$ $p_{\text{perm}}{=}\cnum{k8_perm_p}$; the large gap between Welch and permutation at $K{=}8$ reflects the heavy tail of the PPO distribution ($\sigma/|\mu|{>}9$). Applying Holm--Bonferroni step-down correction~\cite{holm1979} to the family of three tests yields Holm-corrected $p$-values of $\cnum{k4_holm_p}, \cnum{k6_holm_p}, \cnum{k8_holm_p}$ at $K{=}4,6,8$ respectively. \emph{All three $K$-values survive Holm at $\alpha{=}0.05$} after extension to $n{=}\cnum{k4_pcz_seeds},\cnum{k6_pcz_seeds},\cnum{k8_pcz_seeds}$: effect sizes are $d{=}\cnum{k4_welch_d}$ ($K{=}4$), $d{=}\cnum{k6_welch_d}$ ($K{=}6$), $d{=}\cnum{k8_welch_d}$ ($K{=}8$). Benjamini--Hochberg FDR at $q{=}0.05$ also rejects all three. BCa CIs exclude zero at all three $K$-values ($\cnum{k4_welch_ci}$, $\cnum{k6_welch_ci}$, $\cnum{k8_welch_ci}$). We frame the K-scaling result as a \emph{formally significant stability-preservation pattern}: PCZ-PPO's mean varies within a ${\approx}20$-point band across $K{\in}\{4,6,8\}$ while PPO swings ${>}100$ points, with the variance ratio favouring PCZ-PPO at $K{=}4,8$. The Holm rejection strengthens this to a family-wise significant claim. This framing follows the small-$N$ evaluation guidance of Agarwal et al.~\cite{agarwal2021rliable} and the reproducibility discussion of Henderson et al.~\cite{henderson2018deeprl}.

\textbf{Standard deviation convention.} All reported $\pm$ values are \emph{sample} standard deviations ($s$, ddof${}=1$): $s = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N-1}\sum_{i=1}^{N}(x_i - \bar{x})^2}$, following Agarwal et al.~\cite{agarwal2021rliable}. This applies to the mean/std fragments in Tables~\ref{tab:kscaling} and~\ref{tab:ablation}. For the pooled-std denominator of Cohen's $d$ we also use ddof${=}1$, so reported $d$ values are unbiased small-sample estimates. If ddof${=}0$ (population std) were used instead, reported $s$ would be $5{-}18\%$ smaller and reported $d$ correspondingly larger; we flag this explicitly because a previous revision used the ddof${=}0$ convention for Cohen's $d$.

\textbf{Sensitivity findings.} Two results changed direction with additional seeds, demonstrating the importance of adequate seed counts:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{BipedalWalker:} Initially reported as parity ($1.03\times$, 2 seeds). With \cnum{ceBW_pcz_seeds} seeds: $\cnum{ceBW_ratio}\times$ PCZ-PPO advantage ($\cnum{ceBW_pcz_stat}$ vs $\cnum{ceBW_ppo_stat}$). The terminal crash penalty ($-100$) creates sparse/dense mismatch that was masked by the original 2-seed sample.
\item \textbf{K=4 PPO seed sensitivity:} PPO's K=4 performance is highly seed-dependent. At the extended $n{=}\cnum{k4_pcz_seeds}$ canonical sample, multiple PPO seeds fall below reward 100, while few PCZ-PPO seeds do. The 100-threshold is not pre-registered; we pick it as LunarLander's visually-natural break between hovering agents and landing agents. A Mann--Whitney $U$ test on the continuous eval distribution gives $p{=}\cnum{k4_mwu_p}$ (two-sided) without requiring a threshold, supporting the conclusion under a distribution-free test as well.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Effect sizes.} Cohen's $d$ is computed using pooled standard deviation with ddof${=}1$: $d = \frac{\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2}{\sqrt{(s_1^2 + s_2^2)/2}}$. Headline values: $d{=}\cnum{k4_welch_d}$ ($K{=}4$), $d{=}\cnum{k6_welch_d}$ ($K{=}6$), $d{=}\cnum{k8_welch_d}$ ($K{=}8$).

\textbf{Bootstrap confidence intervals.} 95\% CIs are computed via 10{,}000 percentile-bootstrap resamples of the mean difference (fixed seed 20260416). CIs: $\cnum{k4_welch_ci}$ ($K{=}4$), $\cnum{k6_welch_ci}$ ($K{=}6$), $\cnum{k8_welch_ci}$ ($K{=}8$). All three intervals exclude zero under the post-weight-filter data (see ``Run-level deduplication rule'').

\textbf{Robustness checks (BCa, IQM, stochastic dominance).} With heavy-tailed distributions (notably $K{=}8$ PPO), basic-percentile coverage can be optimistic. We therefore re-compute each headline quantity with three complementary tools and report all of them. \emph{(i) BCa bootstrap.} Efron's bias-corrected and accelerated bootstrap (10\,000 resamples, seed 20260416) adjusts for skew and median bias; BCa 95\% CIs on the mean difference: $\cnum{k4_bca_ci}$ ($K{=}4$), $\cnum{k6_bca_ci}$ ($K{=}6$), $\cnum{k8_bca_ci}$ ($K{=}8$). All three exclude zero, but the $K{=}6$ lower bound is very close to zero --- consistent with the weakened $K{=}6$ Welch result after the $n{=}\cnum{k6_pcz_seeds}$ extension. The $K{=}8$ interval shifts rightward relative to percentile, confirming the percentile CI was slightly conservative on the tail side. \emph{(ii) IQM per Agarwal et al.~\cite{agarwal2021rliable}.} Interquartile mean drops the most extreme scores and averages the middle bulk; at $n{=}\cnum{k8_pcz_seeds}{-}\cnum{k4_pcz_seeds}$ this is resistant to single-seed outliers. IQM point estimates (stratified bootstrap 95\% CI, 10\,000 resamples): PCZ-PPO $\cnum{k4_pcz_iqm}$ CI $\cnum{k4_pcz_iqm_ci}$ vs PPO $\cnum{k4_ppo_iqm}$ CI $\cnum{k4_ppo_iqm_ci}$ at $K{=}4$; PCZ-PPO $\cnum{k6_pcz_iqm}$ CI $\cnum{k6_pcz_iqm_ci}$ vs PPO $\cnum{k6_ppo_iqm}$ CI $\cnum{k6_ppo_iqm_ci}$ at $K{=}6$; PCZ-PPO $\cnum{k8_pcz_iqm}$ CI $\cnum{k8_pcz_iqm_ci}$ vs PPO $\cnum{k8_ppo_iqm}$ CI $\cnum{k8_ppo_iqm_ci}$ at $K{=}8$. The PCZ IQM exceeds the PPO IQM at every $K{\in}\{4,6,8\}$, with $K{=}8$ showing the widest separation and the largest PPO CI (consistent with the Welch/BCa analyses). \emph{(iii) Stochastic dominance.} Vargha--Delaney $A_{12}{=}P(X{>}Y)$ with 0.5 weight on ties (a formal test rather than an IQM-figure read-off): $A_{12}{=}\cnum{k4_prob_sup}$ ($K{=}4$), $\cnum{k6_prob_sup}$ ($K{=}6$), $\cnum{k8_prob_sup}$ ($K{=}8$); Mann--Whitney $U$ two-sided $p$-values $\cnum{k4_mwu_p}$ ($K{=}4$), $\cnum{k6_mwu_p}$ ($K{=}6$), $\cnum{k8_mwu_p}$ ($K{=}8$). All four families (mean-based, BCa, IQM, rank-based) agree on the \emph{direction} at every $K$. They agree on \emph{significance} at $K{\in}\{4,8\}$ (MWU $p{<}0.05$, BCa CI excludes zero, IQM CIs nearly non-overlapping); at $K{=}6$, BCa CI excludes zero only marginally and MWU $p{=}\cnum{k6_mwu_p}$, so we report $K{=}6$ as a directional, not tight, finding under the $n{=}\cnum{k6_pcz_seeds}$ extension. The Welch/Holm marginality is disclosed in Table~\ref{tab:kscaling}.

\textbf{Canonical-policy pre-training gate (methodology).} During development we encountered a degenerate-environment failure mode: a synthetic financial-trading environment had an unintended dominant cost-term that drove every learned policy to a trivial hold-cash stationary point, but \emph{both} algorithms being compared (PPO and PCZ-PPO) converged to it, making the subsequent ``tie'' uninformative about algorithmic differences. To prevent this recurrence, we added a \emph{canonical-policy smoke test} (released with the code) which runs an env-specific \emph{trivial} policy (hold-flat / no-op) and an \emph{oracle} policy (informed deterministic baseline) for $N$ episodes each and asserts $\mu_{\text{oracle}} - \mu_{\text{trivial}} > \max(\sigma_{\text{oracle}}, \sigma_{\text{trivial}})$. Environments failing this gate are rejected before any training launches. This is a lightweight version of the ``test-the-testbed-first'' principle from Henderson et al.~\cite{henderson2018deeprl}, and we recommend it as a universal pre-training check for any paper reporting K-scaling or cross-env claims: if the trivial policy is not meaningfully below the informed one, the environment cannot distinguish between algorithms regardless of their relative merits.

The homogeneous variant spans $K{\in}\{2,4,6,8\}$ at 100k/500k with $n{=}5{-}10$ seeds, plus $K{=}4$ extended to 1M ($n{=}\cnum{tv170_n}$) and 5M ($n{=}\cnum{tv172_n}$), and $K{\in}\{2,6,8\}$ extended to 5M ($n{=}\cnum{tv173_n}$); plus a clean three-component decomposition (signed P\&L + transaction fee).

\subsection{LLM Alignment: Single-Step Scope-Boundary (Null)}
\label{app:llm}
\label{sec:llm_alignment}

We ran four LLM regimes on Qwen2.5-0.5B~\cite{qwen2024} + 4-bit QLoRA~\cite{dettmers2023qlora} to check whether the gym findings transfer. The composite reward is a weighted sum of $K$ per-completion signals (sentiment, length, format-compliance, and similar dense scalar rewards); all runs evaluate on 20 held-out prompts and we report total composite reward (mean $\pm$ cross-seed sample std).

\textbf{Conventions used in this section.} \emph{Standard} = sum the $K$ components into a scalar, then globally normalize via running mean/std --- the default RLHF reward pipeline, i.e.\ the LLM-domain analog of the gym aggregate-reward PPO baseline (B1 in Table~\ref{tab:ablation}). \emph{PCZ} = z-normalize each component independently across the rollout, then weighted-sum --- the LLM-domain analog of the gym method introduced in §4. We test both normalizers on top of two policy-gradient algorithms: \emph{PPO} (critic-equipped, with a learned value head and GAE on the normalized reward) and \emph{RLOO}~\cite{ahmadian2024rloo} (REINFORCE Leave-One-Out, critic-free: for each prompt, $G$ completions are sampled and completion $i$'s advantage is its reward minus the mean reward of the remaining $G{-}1$ completions; this is the RLHF-domain analog of the critic-free GRPO baseline used in our gym factorial, §\ref{sec:ablation} cell C1). The 2$\times$2 factorial (regime 4 below) crosses both axes.

Figure~\ref{fig:alignment_results} summarises the two regimes that yielded interpretable comparisons --- the $K{=}6$ 200-vs-500-step regime reversal (left) and the $K{=}4$ 2$\times$2 factorial (right). All four regimes are null or negative for PCZ.

NULL. Both modes learn ($\Delta \approx +2$ over base), but PCZ and standard are indistinguishable at $n{=}3$ (Welch $p{>}0.5$ on eval total). Consistent with the gym $K{=}2$ pattern: $K{=}4$ here has only $K{=}3$ active channels (\texttt{format\_rating} never fires) at similar scales, so PCZ has no scale heterogeneity to exploit.

\section{Reward-Component Independence and Broader Impact}

\textbf{Assumption: approximate component independence.} Per-component z-normalisation is most clearly motivated when the reward components $r^{(k)}$ are approximately statistically independent: the proportionality argument in §\ref{sec:variance_hijacking} relies on the aggregate variance being dominated by a single component's scale. When components are strongly correlated (e.g., LunarLander-K6's \texttt{distance}, \texttt{velocity}, and \texttt{angle} are jointly driven by the lander's dynamics), the per-component ratio is no longer a clean multiplier on the aggregate, and the amplification factor deviates from the independent-component lower bound $\sqrt{\sum_k w_k^2}$ (§\ref{sec:variance_hijacking}). In practice we observe modest deviation rather than failure: the $K{=}6$ post-normalisation standard deviation is roughly 20\% above the independent-component bound (see the signal-tier measurements in §\ref{sec:traditional_rl}), but PCZ-PPO still outperforms aggregate-z-norm PPO at this $K$. Environments with \emph{pathologically} correlated components (e.g., $r^{(1)}{=}r^{(2)}$) are outside the regime where per-component normalisation is meaningful; we do not claim coverage of this degenerate case. The ZCA-whitening variant A8 (Appendix~\ref{app:extended-ablations}) explicitly models the cross-component covariance and is the recommended fallback when component correlation is suspected, but its $K{\times}K$ covariance estimate becomes ill-conditioned at the rollout sample sizes used here for $K{\gtrsim}8$.

\textbf{Broader impact.} PCZ-PPO is a reward-preprocessing method for on-policy RL; its direct societal impact is bounded by the applications in which multi-objective RL is already deployed (robotics, game AI, RLHF alignment). We identify two positive and two negative considerations. \emph{Positive:} (i) explicit per-component weighting makes priority trade-offs \emph{inspectable} --- a safety-critical component appears as an explicit $w_k$ in the configuration rather than being implicit in raw reward scales, which has audit and alignment-governance benefits in domains such as autonomous driving and medical RL; (ii) the reduction in cross-seed variance (especially at high $K$) improves reproducibility of published RL results, addressing a concern documented by Henderson et al.~\cite{henderson2018deeprl}. \emph{Negative:} (i) decoupling the normalisation of individual components enables \emph{independent reward-hacking} --- an agent could game a high-priority sparse component (e.g., a collision penalty) without being constrained by a paired dense component's scale, because the z-normalised version treats each in isolation. Practitioners deploying PCZ-PPO in safety-critical settings should monitor per-component reward trajectories during training for decoupling-induced exploitation, not only aggregate return. (ii) PCZ-PPO requires the user to specify meaningful weights $w_k$; this concentrates design authority in whoever writes the config and, in multi-stakeholder settings (e.g., a self-driving system balancing passenger-comfort, pedestrian-safety, and traffic-flow objectives), the weight vector encodes a contested value judgement. We recommend that deployments in such settings log the weights as part of the model card (\`a la Mitchell et al.) so that priority trade-offs are auditable ex-post. Neither consideration is unique to PCZ-PPO --- they are latent in any multi-objective RL method with explicit scalarisation --- but the explicit per-component normalisation makes them more visible and therefore easier to address.
